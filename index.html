Project overview

Inquiry question

To what extent does the design of this space contribute to positive interactions between human society and the environment?

Objective: To explore the environmental conditions of various urban areas and present the findings through an interactive map.

Hypothesis: The ecological comfort of urban zones is influenced by the amount of greenery, air pollution, and the quality of urban infrastructure.

Format: A website featuring a map that displays data collected from various zones. Users can compare different areas of the city and draw conclusions about their environmental comfort.

ZONE 5

Biology - Alexandra Losavio

Aim

Find out what tree species are planted in Parque das Nações, research their advantages among others, explain why these species were chosen to be planted in the park.

Method

While in the park, take pictures of trees and figure out which ones are more common than others. Using Google, find out the species of most common trees and research their benefits for humans and the environment.

Risk assessments

No potential risks except for basic safety measures (such as watching your step, not getting lost, keeping an eye on the phone, etc.)

Equipment

1 smartphone with access to internet

Results

Most common tree species planted in the park:

  1. European Fan Palm (Chamaerops humilis), recognizable by its fan-shaped leaves and clumping growth habit.
  2. Canary Island Date Palm (Phoenix canariensis), notable for its tall trunk and large crown of feather-like fronds.

Advantages of Chamaerops humilis and Phoenix canariensis:

Palm trees serve as a great ornamental element by giving a tropical and modern flair to the park’s design. They are tall and have relatively small crowns, making them space efficient. Palms are highly resistant to droughts, making them cheaper for maintenance. Apart from that these trees are wind resistant and well-adapted to withstand coastal winds and salt spray.

  1. London Plane Trees (Platanus × acerifolia), which are often planted in urban environments due to their resilience and shade-providing canopy.

Advantages of Platanus × acerifolia:

London Plane Trees tolerate compacted soils, pollution and urban heat making them perfect for the city environment. Their height and shape of their leaves and branches offer excellent shade for the park visitors, cooling down the walkways and public spaces.

  1. Stone Pines (Pinus pinea), also known as umbrella pines. They're very common in Mediterranean climates, including Portugal, and are recognized by their broad, umbrella-shaped canopy.

Advantages of Pinus pinea:

Stone Pines are drought tolerant, which helps them to adapt to Lisbon’s Mediterranean climate with dry summers and appear green and fresh throughout the year. Their tolerance to dry soil also makes them require less irrigation, lowering maintenance costs. Their wide umbrella-like branches offer substantial shade, cooling urban areas and creating a great space for picnics and rest during a hot sunny day. This tree is also native to the region, contributing to a sense of cultural value.

Lab Report: Chemical Analysis of Urban Green Spaces - Pedro Lopes

Parque das Nações Soil and Water Quality Study

Introduction

Understanding the chemical characteristics of urban green spaces is vital for assessing how environments that are efficiently designed promote a better interaction between humans and nature. Soil and water quality directly affect plant growth, biodiversity and public health. In this experiment, soil and water samples were collected from different areas of Parque das Nações, with the objective to assess pH levels and buffering capacity through the litmus test and titration experiments, respectively.

Analysis

pH Interpretation:

  • Water 1 is significantly more acidic, with a pH of 4, indicating possible organic decay, pollution or lack of water circulation in the pond
  • Water 2 is closer to neutral pH, which suggests better conditions, though it is still slightly acidic
  • Both soil samples are slightly acidic, with a pH of 6, which is common for urban soils

Buffering capacity:

  • Soil 1 showed higher resistance to pH changes in comparison to Soil 2, by requiring more base (NaOH) to neutralize, which implies healthier organic matter composition and better maintenance. This is likely due to being near a pavement with regular landscaping
  • Soil 2 and Water 1 had weaker buffering capacity, which makes them more vulnerable to pH changes due to pollution or biological decay.

Evaluation

Several factors may have influenced the accuracy and reliability of the litmus pH test and titrations performed during the investigation. The litmus test uses pH paper, which provides only an approximate reading, meaning that it is less accurate than other methods, such as using a digital pH meter. In addition, the interpretation of the color scale can be subjective. Besides that, the soil pH measurements could've been affected by inconsistent soil to distilled water ratios, which would alter the sample's acidity or alkalinity.

In titration, the measurements can be subjective and imprecise due to how turbid the samples were, which can affect the volume of NaOH needed for a substance to neutralize. Furthermore, the soil samples contained organic matter and particles that interfere with the titration process, which could've deviated the precision and accuracy of what was concluded.

In addition time constraints during lab work could've caused incomplete rinsing of equipment between sets, which contaminates the samples. Ambient temperature could've also affected chemical reactions or caused CO2 absorption, especially in water samples (which would lower their pH levels).

To improve the measurements, using a digital pH meter and doing at least three titrations per sample will improve precision and lower experimental errors.

Conclusion

The analysis of soil and water samples from Parque das Nações reveals that most samples were slightly acidic (with a pH of 6), which is acceptable for urban soils and water, even though it may limit certain types of vegetation. The first water sample, taken from an artificial pond was much more acidic, with a pH of 4 and larger volume of base to neutralize, suggesting poor water circulation and possible organic matter accumulated. In addition, Soil 1 showed better buffering capacity than Soil 2, while Soil 2 and Water 2 had lower buffering capacity.

This experiment reveals that the maintenance of urban green spaces, in this case Parque das Nações, can influence its environment quality, through measurements of pH and its capacity to resist changes (buffering capacity). This indicates that human interaction with the environment, through design, maintenance and infrastructure, has a direct impact on the chemical health of this space.

Well-maintained areas with landscaping and proper drainage, such as of Soil 1, tend to have more stable and resilient soil chemistry, supporting healthier vegetation and biodiversity. In contrast, areas with stagnant water or minimal upkeep, such as those where Water 1 and Soil 2 were collected, are more prone to acidification and less capable of buffering environmental conditions. By assessing these properties, it is possible to understand how thoughtful environmental planning can foster a more sustainable and balanced relationship between human society and the natural world.

Physics

As the physicist in our project, I explored how the physical design of spaces affects interactions between humans and the environment. I focused on measuring sound levels in different areas (e. g., skatepark vs basketball court) and tested how surface friction impacts scooter movement using a ramp. By controlling variables such as weight and ramp angle, i ensured reliable results. The data showed that smoother surfaces reduce friction, allowing easier movement, while noisier areas may affect comfort. I shared all results with the programmer (Ian) to be included in the interactive map. My work helped assess how space design influences environmental experience.
